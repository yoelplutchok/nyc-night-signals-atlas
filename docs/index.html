Executive Summary

Key Finding: Nighttime noise complaint rates vary by a factor of 27 across NYC Community Districts, with the highest-rate district (Bronx CD 12) receiving 259 complaints per 1,000 residents compared to just 9.4 per 1,000 in the lowest-rate district (Staten Island CD 3). This disparity reflects both genuine differences in nighttime disturbance and significant variation in reporting behavior.

Project Scope

This project processes over 2.1 million 311 noise complaints from 2021-2023, filtering to 405,226 nighttime complaints (22:00-07:00), and combines them with environmental sensor data including modeled transportation noise (BTS), satellite light pollution (VIIRS), nighttime temperature (PRISM), and air quality (NYCCAS).

The analysis produces four primary outputs: a Night Noise Atlas with CD-level metrics, a behavioral typology based on complaint patterns, a hotspot concentration analysis at 250m grid resolution, and a heat sensitivity model linking temperature to complaint rates.

Why This Matters

Understanding nighttime noise patterns is essential for urban planning, public health, and environmental justice. Chronic noise exposure during sleep hours is associated with cardiovascular disease, cognitive impairment, and reduced quality of life.

By combining reported complaints with objective environmental measures, this analysis distinguishes between areas with high disturbance and high reporting propensity, identifying both "data deserts" (under-reporting) and "conflict zones" (over-reporting relative to measured exposure).

Principal Findings

1. Extreme Geographic Concentration

  • The top 10 CDs by complaint rate account for 45% of all nighttime noise complaints despite containing only 23% of NYC's population. Manhattan and Bronx CDs dominate the high-complaint end.
  • Residential noise is the dominant complaint type (47%), followed by street/sidewalk (22%) and vehicle noise (9%). Helicopter complaints, while only 5% of total, show distinct geographic patterns concentrated in helicopter corridors.
  • Weekend nights (Friday/Saturday) generate 32% higher complaint volume than weekday nights, with the uplift most pronounced in entertainment districts.

2. Behavioral Typology

  • Nine distinct behavioral clusters emerge from K-means clustering on complaint rate, weekend uplift, late-night share, and warm-season ratio. These range from "Low Activity Residential" to "High Volume Mixed Entertainment" profiles.
  • Temporal stability analysis shows 62% of CDs maintain the same cluster assignment across all three years (2021-2023), indicating structural behavioral patterns rather than transient phenomena.
  • NTA-level analysis (197 residential neighborhoods) reveals finer-grained variation within CDs, with some CDs containing multiple behavioral types.

3. Hotspot Persistence

  • At 250m grid resolution, 19 cells appear in the top 100 hotspots in all three years, indicating persistent structural noise sources rather than episodic events.
  • Artifact flagging identifies likely data quality issues: cells with >95% of complaints from a single coordinate or >200 complaints on a single day are flagged as suspected serial complainants or data entry errors.
  • Gini coefficient analysis shows complaint concentration within CDs varies from 0.45 to 0.85, with higher values indicating complaints clustered at specific locations rather than distributed across the district.

4. Heat-Complaint Association

  • Citywide, each 1C increase in nighttime minimum temperature is associated with a 2.3% increase in noise complaints, after controlling for day-of-week, month, and year effects.
  • Heat sensitivity varies significantly across CDs, ranging from near-zero to 5%+ per degree, with higher sensitivity in areas with more residential complaints.
  • The 2021-2023 period included 9,628 "hot nights" (Tmin >= 20C) across all CDs, representing potential high-disturbance conditions.

5. Equity Implications

  • Higher poverty rates and Hispanic composition correlate positively with objective environmental burden (r = +0.22 and +0.26 respectively), while Black composition shows a different spatial pattern.
  • East Harlem (Manhattan CD 5) emerges as the most environmentally burdened district, ranking #1 on the objective Environmental Sleep Index with the highest air pollution levels in NYC.
  • 10 CDs are classified as "data deserts" with high objective noise but low complaint rates, suggesting under-reporting in outer borough residential areas.

Methodology

The analysis follows a reproducible pipeline architecture with 14 scripts, centralized configuration, metadata sidecars for all outputs, and over 430 automated tests. All timestamps are converted to NYC timezone (America/New_York) with DST-safe handling.

Data Acquisition

311 complaints fetched via NYC Open Data API (2.1M records). Environmental rasters from BTS (transportation noise), NASA VIIRS (light), Oregon State PRISM (temperature), and NYC DOHMH NYCCAS (air quality). All raw data checksummed and manifest-tracked.

Spatial Processing

Complaints geocoded and assigned to 59 residential Community Districts using hardened spatial joins (within-first, then nearest with 500ft threshold). CRS handling enforced: EPSG:4326 for storage, EPSG:2263 (NY State Plane) for distance/area operations.

Feature Engineering

Nighttime filtering (22:00-07:00), time-bin classification (evening/early_am/core_night/predawn), weekend identification (Fri/Sat nights), warm-season flagging (Jun-Aug). Rates normalized by population and area. Environmental exposures computed via zonal statistics with energy-averaged dB for noise.

Clustering & Analysis

K-means clustering (k=9) on standardized features with reproducibility verification. Poisson GLM for heat sensitivity with categorical controls. Population-weighted aggregation for tract-to-CD crosswalks using intersection populations.

Validation & QA

CDC PLACES sleep data for ecological validation (with SAE circularity caveat). Negative control (mammography) confirms no spurious correlations. 430+ unit tests covering schema validation, algorithmic correctness, and edge cases.

Reproducibility

This project follows a strict reproducibility protocol with 20 guardrails covering path resolution, CRS handling, atomic writes, provenance tracking, and deterministic outputs.

Pipeline Architecture

  • 14 numbered scripts executed in sequence, each with JSONL structured logging
  • Metadata sidecars for all outputs capturing input hashes, git commit, runtime environment, and QA statistics
  • Centralized configuration in YAML files for time windows, thresholds, and index weights
  • Atomic file operations using temp-file-then-rename pattern to prevent corrupted state on failure

Test Coverage

  • 430+ automated tests covering schema validation, value bounds, algorithmic correctness, and edge cases
  • CD labeling policy enforcement with hard failure on any inconsistency between computed and stored labels
  • Spatial join hardening with logged distance distributions and <1% unmatched threshold
  • Reproducibility verification for clustering with re-run comparison
